## Executive Brief

South Africa's insurance market is not *exposed to* political risk — it is *structured as* a political risk. Within a single week in May 2026, the Constitutional Court reserved judgment on the NHI Act (7 May) and revived the Phala Phala impeachment process against President Ramaphosa (8 May). The industry is treating these as sequential political events requiring monitoring. They are not. They are a correlated fragility trigger that the sector's actuarial models, capital buffers, and strategic plans have not been built to absorb simultaneously.

**Why this week:** ConCourt judgment on NHI public participation is now pending — weeks to months away — while the impeachment committee process runs in parallel, testing GNU cohesion in real time ahead of the 2026 municipal elections. The SARB's April 2026 Monetary Policy Review has simultaneously shifted to an Iran war baseline with a severe scenario projecting a repo rate of 8.17% and inflation at 4.56%, while the rand trades near R17/$. Three compound shocks — constitutional, political, and macro — are not yet priced into any sub-sector's fragility assessment.

**Who wins, who loses:** Political risk underwriters and gap cover providers gain, because demand spikes when uncertainty rises. Life insurers and commercial property underwriters lose, because their investment portfolios are exposed to a rate reversal scenario that was categorised as tail risk six months ago and is now the SARB's intermediate case. Medical schemes are the sector most binary in their exposure: NHI invalidation restores structural certainty; NHI validation accelerates an existential restructuring for which most schemes have incomplete transition plans.

## The Contrarian Take

The medical scheme industry will win its legal battle against NHI — and the victory will accelerate the industry's structural decline faster than NHI implementation ever would have. Here is the arithmetic: **medical scheme membership has fallen by approximately 200,000–250,000 beneficiaries annually for five consecutive years, entirely independent of NHI legislation.** Premium increases running at 8–10% versus income growth of 4–6% are pricing the middle class out of private healthcare at a rate no court ruling can reverse. An industry that spends five years and hundreds of millions of rand on legal strategy while the affordability problem continues unchecked has won the battle by winning the wrong battle.

The specific error is a category confusion: the industry has defined "survival" as legal preservation of the medical schemes framework. **Legal preservation is not the same as market preservation.** A medical scheme industry that is legally intact but shrinking to a high-income niche of 4–5 million beneficiaries — down from 9 million today — has not survived NHI. It has survived the law while losing to economics.

The strategic implication is urgent: every rand spent on NHI litigation that is not simultaneously invested in genuine affordability innovation — lower-cost benefit options, employer co-payment renegotiation, provider network repricing — is a rand spent defending a legal status that the market is already voting to exit. **The most dangerous outcome for medical schemes is not NHI. It is winning the NHI case and then discovering that the membership exodus was never about NHI at all.**

## Closing Line

South Africa's insurance industry is so focused on watching the constitutional scaffolding shake that it has stopped checking whether the building it protects is still worth saving.
